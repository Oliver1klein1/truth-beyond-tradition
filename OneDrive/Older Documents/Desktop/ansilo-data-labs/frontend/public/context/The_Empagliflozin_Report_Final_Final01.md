# Executive Summary

Empagliflozin was presented to the medical world **as a breakthrough**: **a diabetes drug associated with a striking 38% reduction in cardiovascular death in the EMPA-REG OUTCOME trial**.

That claim mattered enormously. It helped elevate the drug into a major clinical and commercial asset, shaped professional enthusiasm, and contributed to its later stabilization within mainstream treatment narratives. This report examines whether that narrative ***became cleaner, stronger, and more certain than the underlying record justified.***

The central finding is **not** that empagliflozin lacks value. Later evidence **supports meaningful benefits, particularly in heart failure and kidney disease**.

The central finding is narrower: **the original mortality story appears more fragile than the dominant narrative often suggested**. The report identifies multiple points of tension around EMPA-REG, including the unusually rapid timing of the claimed benefit, uncertainty around the classification of key deaths, regional inconsistency, stroke-related unease, and the absence of a convincing dose-response pattern. Taken together, these issues do not automatically invalidate the trial, but they do weaken the impression of a clean, decisive mortality miracle.

The report also finds that the narrative was not stabilized mainly by *detached external confirmation*. Much of the later literature that defended, extended, or biologically explained the original claim emerged from **a sponsor-linked interpretive ecosystem**. Mechanistic explanations, post hoc reframings, and supportive analyses often helped smooth over unresolved tensions rather than decisively settle them. The report’s authorship audit makes that pattern visible: across the core papers used to stabilize the empagliflozin story, **financially aligned authors account for 83% to 100% of named authors in the sample shown.**

**Regulators** occupy an especially important position in this story. They are not pharmaceutical companies, and this report does not treat them as such. But regulators often make decisions on the basis of evidence packages assembled by sponsors, under conditions of uncertainty, time pressure, and practical constraint. In the empagliflozin case, the report argues that regulatory acceptance and independent clinical certainty were not the same thing. Once a narrow and uneasy approval was granted, *the wider medical system had a strong tendency to interpret that decision as stronger confirmation than it may actually have represented*.

**This report therefore does not begin from a presumption of fraud or bad faith by regulators, medical experts, or the companies involved.** It begins from a different concern: that under modern evidence conditions, incomplete data, post hoc explanation, institutional incentives, and repeated sponsor-shaped interpretation can harden uncertainty into **apparent consensus**.

Empagliflozin is clearly a useful drug. But the record examined here suggests that the celebrated mortality narrative was more contestable, more patched, and less independently stabilized than later clinical confidence often implied.

Narrative friction usually emerges *gradually* rather than dramatically. A result is reported. A compelling interpretation is attached to it. Over time, what began as a contested or incomplete finding *may harden into a familiar clinical narrative*. Once that happens, the narrative itself can begin to shape what later readers notice, question, or *regard as already settled*.

Several recurring features of the evidence ecosystem can contribute to this process.<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image7.png" style="width:3.35851in;height:3.74649in" />

# The EMPA-REG Outcome Trial

## When the Treatment of Diabetes Changed

Empagliflozin is a diabetes drug that was presented to the world as something close to a medical miracle. When the trial results were first announced, the headline claim was extraordinary: a 38% reduction in cardiovascular death.\[1\]\[2\] In a field where clinicians had struggled for decades to reduce cardiovascular mortality in people with diabetes, that claim landed like a thunderclap. The audience in Stockholm at the European Association for the Study of Diabetes (EASD) 2015 Annual Meeting applauded wildly and rose to their feet as one.\[3\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image13.png" style="width:4.6791in;height:2.98943in" />

Figure : An impressive result - 38% reduction in CV Death

But when you look more closely, serious questions begin to emerge. Such as ‘Why do those taking the drug have more strokes than those who don’t?’ \[4\]

One person who did look closer ended up writing the following paper:

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image14.png" style="width:6.6in;height:1.66597in" />

Figure : Alzaid A. Empa's New Clothes: The Untold Story of the Empa-Reg Outcome Trial.

Dr Alzaid was in the room where the data was being presented.\[3\]\[4\] He quickly noticed that the story being sold didn’t match the data. And those questions are not minor technicalities. They go to the very heart of **how drugs are evaluated, how regulators make decisions**, and how the medical establishment can end up defending a narrative ***that may rest on shaky foundations***. This report follows **two major lines of inquiry**.

1)  First, we are looking at the possibility that **the EMPA-REG result was**, at least in part, **a** **statistical illusion**: a result that appears powerful on the surface but becomes much less convincing when the biology, the math, and the trial design are examined carefully.

2)  Second, we are asking **how the pharmaceutical and academic ecosystem responded once those cracks became visible**. Who defended the trial? What kinds of explanations were offered? And how much of that defense came from structurally independent science versus sponsor-funded narratives?

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image15.png" style="width:0.93681in;height:1.63264in" />To guide that second part of the discussion, the report will use a simple traffic-light framework.

**Green** means structurally independent: **no declared financial conflicts** and no direct sponsor control.

**Amber** means mixed: often academic experts operating within a system where **industry funding, consulting, or honoraria** are common.

**Red** means **direct pharmaceutical influence**: sponsor-funded research, sponsor-controlled analyses, or authors employed by the company marketing the drug.

This matters because most people assume that when a doctor prescribes a drug based on “the evidence,” that evidence is the product of **a neutral, objective scientific process.** But sometimes the scientific scaffolding supporting a drug looks less like pure discovery and more like a coordinated and heavily-funded campaign of narrative reinforcement.

# Why the EMPA-REG result mattered so much

To understand why EMPA-REG created such excitement, you must understand the background. For decades, medicine faced a grim reality: **nearly two-thirds of people with diabetes** died from cardiovascular complications.\[5\] Strokes, myocardial infarctions, and other vascular events drove enormous morbidity and mortality.

And despite all the focus on glucose control, lowering blood sugar alone **did not reliably translate into fewer cardiovascular deaths**.\[6\] Correcting blood sugar did not stop the heart attacks from happening.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image16.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.68681in" />

Figure : Controlling blood sugar alone does not improve CV outcomes

That was the frustration. Doctors could improve glucose numbers, yet patients still died from heart disease. Trials targeting hyperglycemia repeatedly failed to demonstrate the kind of cardiovascular mortality benefit clinicians wanted.

So when EMPA-REG appeared to show a dramatic reduction in cardiovascular death, **the response was explosive.** The room was electrified.\[1\]\[3\] A thirty-eight percent reduction is a massive number in literally any field of medicine. But in this specific context, diabetes and heart disease, it was just unheard of.\[1\]\[2\]

It was described as a **breakthrough, a game changer,** even **the holy grail of diabetes care.**  
But as Dr. Alzaid argued in his paper, the triumphal narrative did not sit comfortably with the actual details of the data.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image17.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.63681in" />

Figure : Closer inspection reveals 5 striking anomalies

Specifically, there were 5 areas that did not sit comfortably.

## The first major crack: The biology moved too fast

One of the first red flags was the speed at which **the survival curves separated.  
**Cardiovascular disease, driven by atherosclerosis, is a slow process. Plaque builds up **over years**, often decades. In most cardiovascular outcome trials, if a treatment is genuinely modifying that underlying disease process, you would expect meaningful divergence in outcomes only after a substantial period of time, often 12 to 18 months or longer.

But in EMPA-REG, the curves began separating **within weeks**.\[1\]\[4\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image18.png" style="width:6.08926in;height:3.15995in" />

Figure : Biology logic appears to be defied

That is hard to reconcile with the biology. It is worth repeating this to fully appreciate the ramifications. If a house has twenty years of structural termite damage, you do not spray something on the foundation and expect it to withstand a category-five storm two weeks later. The structural problem is already deeply established.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image19.png" style="width:5.8481in;height:3.27848in" />

Figure : The Impossible Timeline

So the speed of the benefit immediately raises a question: if the drug was not plausibly reversing chronic vascular disease that quickly, then what exactly was it doing?

<span id="_Toc225996008" class="anchor"></span>**The post hoc biological explanations**

Once the timing problem became obvious, defenders of the trial needed a biological explanation that could account for the rapid benefit. Several theories emerged.

One was **the “thrifty substrate” hypothesis**: the idea that the drug somehow shifts the heart’s fuel use from glucose to ketones, thereby improving cardiac efficiency. The idea was that this switch somehow made the heart operate more efficiently and prevented it from failing.\[8\]

Another theory was the **diuretic or hemodynamic explanation**: the idea that the drug rapidly reduces fluid volume and sodium, lowering circulatory burden and taking stress off a failing heart.\[7\]  
To support that second explanation, researchers promoted **mediation analyses**, especially around changes in hematocrit.\[9\] A mediation analysis is essentially a mathematical way of proving that variable A causes variable B by passing through a specific middleman, which is variable C. In this case variable C is the hematocrit … the concentration of red blood cells. They analyzed the data and argued that as the water was flushed out of the patients' systems, ***their hematocrit naturally went up***. They used this statistical middleman to declare that the water pill theory was the primary driver of the rapid survival benefit. Volume reduction was the key pathway behind the survival benefit.\[9\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image20.png" style="width:5.85506in;height:3.44009in" />

Figure : CV benefits appeared to be independent of risk factors.

But even if you accept those explanations provisionally, they do not cleanly solve the deeper contradictions. For example, how does a drug supposedly prevent cardiovascular death without meaningfully preventing the underlying non-fatal events such as stroke or myocardial infarction?\[1\]\[4\] How does it lower blood pressure, yet still show troubling stroke patterns in some regional data?\[1\]\[4\] How does it act through a strong physiological mechanism, yet show essentially no meaningful dose-response difference between 10 mg and 25 mg?\[1\]\[4\]

These mechanistic theories were not a decisive explanation. They were, at best, attempts to retrofit a story around an already-publicized result.

### Who built those explanations?

This is where the conflict-of-interest lens becomes so important.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image21.png" style="width:1.10833in;height:1.90972in" />The major mechanistic defenses were not, for the most part, the product of detached, structurally independent investigators. Many were **red**: directly sponsor-funded, based on sponsor-controlled data, or authored in part by scientists employed by the companies marketing the drug.

Others were **amber**: written by prominent academic experts whose work and influence exist within a broader pharmaceutical-funded ecosystem.

That does not automatically make every conclusion false. But it does matter. If the biological “patches” used to explain an implausible timeline come primarily from the manufacturer and its network of allied key opinion leaders, then those explanations deserve closer scrutiny, not automatic trust.

## The statistical core of the problem: Non-assessable deaths

Now we come to mathematics, and this is where things become especially uncomfortable.

Now we come to mathematics, and this is where things become especially uncomfortable.  
The trial’s claim of reduced cardiovascular death depended heavily on **how deaths were classified.** In large global trials, not every death can be definitively adjudicated. Sometimes the records are incomplete. Sometimes there is no autopsy. Sometimes the available evidence is simply insufficient to determine the exact cause.

In EMPA-REG, a substantial fraction of the cardiovascular deaths was **non-assessable**.\[4\]\[10\] In plain language, the researchers could not confidently determine **whether those patients truly died of cardiovascular causes.**

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image22.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.65139in" />

Figure : CV benefits disappears when ‘mystery deaths’ removed.

And yet those deaths were still counted as cardiovascular deaths under the trial’s adjudication framework.\[4\]\[10\] Because this specific trial was focused on tracking cardiovascular outcomes, the researchers just presumed that these mystery deaths were cardiovascular in origin. They just lumped them into the final tally. The uncomfortable truth is that when these mystery deaths were removed, the P value spikes, indicating that any remaining difference between the drug and the placebo is essentially indistinguishable from random noise.\[4\]\[10\]

**That matters enormously**. Because when regulators examined what happened after removing those uncertain cases from the analysis, the dramatic mortality advantage was no longer persuasive.\[10\] The signal weakened substantially, and the apparent miracle looked far less secure.  
**This is one of the most serious concerns in the whole trial.** If a major headline benefit depends heavily on deaths whose causes were never clearly established, the result becomes much more fragile than the public narrative suggests.

## The geographic anomaly

Then there is the regional inconsistency.

If a drug is truly acting through a robust and universal biological mechanism, you would expect broadly consistent benefit across human populations. But in EMPA-REG, the data were not evenly convincing across regions. \[4\]

A large share, 61%, of the trial population came from **Europe and North America**, yet those regions **did not show the same clear mortality benefit that made the global result so famous**. Much of the positive effect appeared to come from smaller cohorts in **Asia and Latin America**. \[4\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image23.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.70556in" />

Figure : No CV death benefit in Europe and North America - 61% of the trial population

That is difficult to ignore.

Imagine a group project with ten students. Six contribute very little, but four perform brilliantly. The average grade may still look impressive, yet the average hides the uneven reality underneath.

That is the concern here. A global headline can obscure major regional inconsistencies. And once regional inconsistency is present, the natural question becomes: **are we looking at a universal biological effect, or at some mix of chance, measurement differences, local practice variation, and analytical reconstruction?**

## The stroke paradox

Another issue is the so-called **stroke paradox**.

The drug was celebrated for reducing cardiovascular death, yet it did not convincingly reduce the underlying non-fatal components of cardiovascular disease in the way one might expect. \[1\]\[4\] In some regional analyses, **stroke rates were not improved and may even have looked worse**. \[4\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image24.png" style="width:6.01892in;height:3.47684in" />

Figure : The Stroke Paradox

That creates a conceptual problem. A treatment that truly modifies vascular disease should not merely make strokes or heart attacks less deadly while leaving their occurrence untouched. The idea that it somehow does not **stop the gun from firing, but does stop the bullet from being lethal, is biologically awkward.**

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image25.png" style="width:6.6in;height:4.03611in" />

Figure : Preventing death but not the illness leading to the death.

And this problem becomes even more uncomfortable when paired with ***improved blood pressure and glucose metrics.*** If those variables moved in the expected direction, why did the event pattern not line up more cleanly with the survival claim?

Empagliflozin was celebrated for preventing death from cardiovascular disease, but it had absolutely no effect on preventing the individual non-fatal components of the disease. Like non-fatal strokes or myocardial infarctions. How does a drug stop you from dying of a heart attack or a stroke without actually stopping you from having the heart attack in the first place? If it's truly treating the atherosclerotic disease, shouldn't both the non-fatal events and the fatal events decrease together?

Conceptually, it is incredibly difficult to reconcile. It's as if the drug doesn't stop the gun from firing, it just magically stops the bullet from being lethal once it hits you. A drug cannot preferentially target only the complications that end in death while completely ignoring the complications the patient actually survives. Especially when you consider the blood pressure data. Patients taking empagliflozin had significantly lower blood pressure than those in the placebo group. \[1\]

We know that lower blood pressure reduces your risk of stroke. **Yet despite having lower blood pressure and better blood sugar control, the patients on the drug had numerically *<u>more</u>* strokes**. \[1\]\[4\]

## The missing dose-response relationship

Then there is the failure of a basic pharmacological expectation: **dose response**.

In pharmacology, if a drug is directly causing an effect through its active mechanism, a higher dose often produces a stronger effect, or at least a measurably different one. If a drug is actively causing a specific biological reaction, giving a patient a higher dose of that drug should generally cause a stronger reaction. In the same way that one shot of an espresso increases your heart rate a little bit. Three shots increase it a lot. That graded response is a very strong indicator of causality. It proves that the espresso, or in this case, the drug, is what's actually driving the effect.

But EMPA-REG used two doses: **10 mg** and **25 mg**. And the outcome curves for those groups were remarkably similar. \[1\]\[4\]\[12\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image26.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.94514in" />

Figure : No dose-response relationship.

That does not sit comfortably with the mechanistic explanations offered after the fact. (Neither does this sit comfortably with the fact that these doses are priced differently, which implies a dose-response.)

If the drug was saving lives because of a powerful diuretic effect or because it was transforming myocardial fuel utilization, one would expect some visible difference between a lower and a much higher dose. **The near-superimposable curves weaken the argument that the claimed benefit was clearly driven by the drug’s intended biological action**.

## So, to summarize the anomalies:

We have a **biological timeline** that is **impossibly fast**.

We have **mechanism theories** that appear to be unsupported **guesswork**.

We have data heavily padded by **unknown causes of death**, which, when removed, destroy the statistical significance.

We have massive **geographic inconsistencies** where the majority of the trial saw **no benefit.**

We have the logical **paradox** where the drug **prevents death**, but **not** the actual **heart attacks**, or even the **strokes** which usually lead to the deaths, with **zero dose-response** curve to back any of it up. We have **strokes increasing** in Europe despite blood pressure lowering and blood glucose control improving. This leaves us with a mountain of **scientific and statistical uncertainty**.

## Was this a benefit trial, or a safety trial repurposed after the fact?

Which brings this whole story to the doorstep of the FDA.

To understand the FDA's role here, you have to understand a crucial piece of regulatory history. In 2008, the FDA issued a sweeping mandate for all new diabetes drugs.\[13\] It was in response to a major scandal involving a different diabetes drug, rosiglitazone or Avandia.\[13\]\[14\] That drug was found to successfully lower blood sugar, but *it simultaneously increased the risk of heart attacks*.\[14\] The FDA realized that just lowering blood sugar wasn't enough if the drug was actively killing patients in other ways. So they mandated that **all future diabetes drugs must undergo massive long-term cardiovascular outcome trials.\[13\]**

But, and this is the critical distinction, these were mandated as **<u>safety trials</u>**. The main objective was to show that a new drug did not raise cardiovascular risk beyond an acceptable threshold.\[13\]

**That distinction matters.**

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image27.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.94444in" />

Figure : The CV Death finding was exploratory, not primary, based on how the study was designed.

EMPA-REG was designed to show safety, not originally to prove a **cardiovascular mortality benefit** as its primary efficacy target.\[10\]\[13\] The famous mortality signal emerged in a context that, from a statistical and regulatory perspective, this was **an exploratory finding**.\[4\]\[10\]

Meaning they didn't set out to look for it. They just gathered a mountain of safety data, ran it through a computer after the fact, and looked to see if any positive trends happened to pop up.

And exploratory findings can *generate hypotheses*. They can *point toward future research*. But **they do not carry the same evidentiary weight as a trial designed from the ground up to test that exact claim.**

It is a bit like firing an arrow into a barn wall and then painting the bullseye around it afterward. This CV death finding was meant to be a tip-off for a future trial, **not scientific proof of efficacy**. However, a dedicated cardiovascular outcomes trial takes years to design, enroll, and execute, and it costs hundreds of millions of dollars. If a pharmaceutical company can convince the FDA to accept an exploratory analysis from a repurposed safety trial as proof of efficacy, they save an absolute fortune and get their claim to market years faster.

And there's a massive danger in doing this, particularly regarding **who the drug is eventually prescribed to**.

The trial enrolled a highly unique, severely *sick group of elderly patients.*

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image28.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.96181in" />

Figure : The Trial Population vs the general public, the everyday clinic patient.

More than 75% of them had clinical evidence of advanced ischemic heart disease.\[1\] Meaning their arteries were already severely narrowed, starving the heart muscle of oxygen. And nearly a quarter of them had already suffered a stroke in the past.\[1\] This is a population crippled by advanced late-stage atherosclerotic disease. If you walk into a standard everyday diabetes clinic, less than five percent of the patients have a history of a stroke.

## The FDA Split Decision: the 12–11 vote

When the FDA advisory committee reviewed the evidence, **the decision was anything but unanimous.** Taking all the evidence into account: the biological flaws, the non-assessable deaths, the geographic skew, and the fact that this was fundamentally just a repurposed safety trial, the FDA still had to make a decision. The FDA convened an advisory board of top medical and statistical experts to review this exact data. They had to vote on whether to approve empagliflozin specifically for the prevention of cardiovascular deaths.\[11\]

The final vote was 12 to 11 in favor.\[11\] That is **extraordinarily close**. That is razor thin. On a knife-edge. Remember these results received a standing ovation in Stockholm\![3\]

It means **the expert panel was almost split down the middle.**

And that fact alone should temper the certainty of the triumphal story. **When nearly half of an expert panel reviewing the same data is unconvinced, that is not a portrait of settled science**. It is a portrait of contested evidence. Nearly half of the medical jury looked at the data and essentially rejected the application. They were either fundamentally in doubt of the evidence or deeply uncomfortable with the statistical methodology.

If half the mechanics in a shop tell you your brakes are dangerously faulty and the other half say they are acceptable, you do not leave with serene confidence. You ask for more testing. You seek more clarity. You recognize uncertainty.

Yet in medicine, once an approval is granted, **uncertainty often gets flattened into a much more confident public message.**

When we look at how to view regulatory performance critically, it is clear that **an FDA approval isn't a guarantee of scientific truth.**

**The regulators versus the independent reviewers**

Regulators like the FDA and EMA are not the same thing as pharmaceutical companies. Structurally, they are **green** in the sense that they are public institutions rather than commercial manufacturers.

So in a sense, they are **independent judges working from evidence assembled largely by the party seeking approval.**

That is why some fully independent groups stood out so sharply when they reviewed the same material. Bodies such as *Germany’s IQWiG (Institute for Quality and Efficiency in Health Care)* and independent academic critics focused much more directly on the uncertainties: **non-assessable deaths**, **regional inconsistency**, **stroke signals**, and **the fragility of the mortality claim**.

## How the narrative was defended after approval

Once regulators **allowed the cardiovascular mortality claim through**, the institutional machinery shifted quickly.

**Clinical guidelines** from major professional bodies elevated the drug. Mechanistic reviews amplified sponsor-friendly explanations. Subgroup analyses and alternative statistical framings were used to argue that the benefit was consistent and robust.

One important example involved **post hoc analytical reframing**: instead of focusing only on time-to-first-event, later analyses could shift attention toward total burden of events, recurrent events, or specially chosen subgroup patterns.

And this is precisely why the funding question matters. Many of these subsequent defenses were not neutral replications by independent investigators.

# What do we see when we analyze the clinical data published AFTER EMPA-REG, between 2015 and 2026?

What became of the medical miracle announced in Stockholm in 2015 … the daily pill that stops cardiovascular death by a staggering 38%?

If you were waiting for the highly anticipated sequel to this blockbuster movie, you would have noticed that, without warning, the genre has completely shifted. It is no longer a high-stakes thriller about **cheating death**. It is now more like a mild, quiet documentary about **preventing hospital visits**.  
That raises a serious question: what happens to the laws of medical science when a supposed miracle cure simply refuses to repeat itself?

That question forces us to look behind the curtain and see **how medical evidence is actually arrived at**. Because when a claim that massive fails to replicate, you are no longer just looking at biology. You are looking at **the architecture of the clinical trial industry and the pharma-regulatory ecosystem itself**.

The data on empagliflozin since 2015 includes massive follow-up trials like **EMPEROR-Reduced**, **EMPEROR-Preserved**, and **EMPA-KIDNEY**.\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]

Does the post-Empa-reg trial data *echo the anomalies* pointed out earlier, or does this data *nullify the critique entirely* and prove **the 38% miracle was real all along.**

This is the ultimate reality check.

Understanding how the medical establishment tests a drug after a highly controversial blockbuster claim is critical if we are to make scientific progress.

The pharmaceutical sponsors, Boehringer Ingelheim and Eli Lilly, knew they could not rest on a controversial single trial, controversial because of the 12-11 split vote at the FDA.\[11\] They needed more data to solidify the drug’s place in the market.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image37.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.41736in" />

Figure : The Empagliflozin Clinical Trial Direction

But instead of running another trial focused purely on **cardiovascular death in diabetic patients**, they pivoted hard. They launched the **EMPEROR program**, testing the drug on entirely different populations, specifically patients with heart failure.\[15\]\[16\]\[18\]

Strategically, that makes sense. If the FDA vote was essentially a coin toss on the mortality claim alone, running another pure mortality trial would be an enormous financial risk. Hundreds of millions of dollars would be on the line.

So they cast a wider net. And what happened when they tested it on heart failure patients?  
In the EMPEROR-Reduced trial, which focused on patients whose hearts are not pumping out enough blood, the drug showed a strong benefit. It reduced its primary composite endpoint from 24.7% down to 19.4%.\[15\] In EMPEROR-Preserved, which looked at patients whose hearts are too stiff to fill properly, they saw a solid 21% relative reduction.\[16\]

That sounds impressive, but the phrase “**primary composite endpoint”** is doing an enormous amount of heavy lifting in those statistics.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image38.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.39444in" />

Figure : The Composite Endpoint Strategy

A composite endpoint is essentially **a bundle**. It combines several very different medical outcomes into one statistical bucket. In these trials, the researchers combined **cardiovascular death** with **heart failure hospitalizations**. That means a patient dying of a heart attack and another patient going to the hospital for a few days to manage fluid retention are counted **as equal events** in the final math.\[15\]\[16\]\[18\]  
Clinically, however, heart failure *hospitalizations* are **common**, while actual *cardiovascular* *deaths* are **much rarer** during the short timeframe of a trial. So when you bundle those outcomes together, ***the hospitalizations dominate the statistics***. The drug looks highly successful, but mostly because it is preventing hospital visits.\[15\]\[16\]

And once that bucket is unbundled, once the hospitalizations are stripped away and the cardiovascular deaths are isolated, the miraculous 38% reduction from the original EMPA-REG trial **disappears**.  
In the EMPEROR trials, when you look purely at cardiovascular death, the hazard ratios cluster around 0.91 and 0.92.\[15\]\[16\] In plain language, that translates to roughly an 8% relative reduction. But the confidence intervals show that this reduction is not statistically significant. The drug did not clearly save lives in those heart failure trials.\[15\]\[16\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image39.png" style="width:5.91929in;height:4.25636in" />

Figure : Cardiovascular Death in the Emperor Trials

They widened the net again with EMPA-KIDNEY, enrolling patients with chronic kidney disease and using yet another composite endpoint: **kidney disease progression or death from cardiovascular causes.** That composite was positive, with a 28% relative risk reduction (hazard ratio 0.72, 95% CI 0.64–0.82). \[17\]

But that result still does not recreate the original EMPA-REG mortality story. EMPA-KIDNEY did not report a standalone cardiovascular-death hazard ratio in the way the EMPEROR trials did, so it cannot be presented as a direct replication of the earlier cardiovascular-death signal. In the EMPEROR follow-up trials, the hazard ratios for cardiovascular death were 0.92 in EMPEROR-Reduced and 0.91 in EMPEROR-Preserved, neither conventionally statistically significant on its own. \[17\]

So across the board, **none of these massive follow-up trials reproduced the headline-grabbing 38% reduction in cardiovascular death seen in EMPA-REG**. What they did show was something more measured but ***still clinically important***: *empagliflozin helps reduce heart-failure hospitalizations and slows kidney-disease progression.* That is meaningful medicine, but it is a long way from the dramatic mortality miracle that originally captured international attention.

So does that mean Al-Zaid was entirely right? Was the original 38% simply a mathematical illusion, and the drug essentially a placebo when it comes to keeping people alive?

The answer is nuanced.

The new data softens the strongest form of the critique - the idea that the drug might be a total mirage. The SGLT2 inhibitor class, which includes empagliflozin, genuinely improves heart and kidney outcomes. That part is real.\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]

But to understand why the original trial looked like a miracle and the new trials look so different, it is necessary to revisit the argument about **biological implausibility**.

Al-Zaid argued that atherosclerosis - the process of plaque building up in the arteries -is glacial. It takes decades of diet, smoking, genetics, and metabolic damage to clog those pipes. Yet in the original EMPA-REG trial, **the survival curve separated almost immediately**. Patients taking the drug appeared to stop dying within weeks. His argument was straightforward: you cannot reverse decades of plaque buildup in three weeks. That is biologically impossible.\[4\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image40.png" style="width:6.6in;height:4.55625in" />

Figure : A plausible mechanism

Initially, the medical community struggled to explain that rapid benefit. But the post-EMPA-REG data, especially the EMPEROR trials and related mediation analyses, gradually offered a more biologically plausible explanation.\[7\]\[9\]\[15\]\[16\]

The later evidence suggests that the early benefit is unlikely to reflect a rapid reversal of plaque biology. Instead, it is more plausibly explained by hemodynamic effects—changes in blood flow, filling pressures, and fluid distribution that can occur far more quickly.\[7\]\[9\]

In mediation analyses, researchers found that **changes in hematocrit and hemoglobin -** biological proxies for plasma volume, or how concentrated the blood is - accounted for as much as 85% to 95% of the risk reduction.\[9\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image41.png" style="width:5.93142in;height:3.15419in" />

Figure : The hemodynamic mechanism appears to be supported by hematocrit changes

As we saw previously, a useful analogy is this: Al-Zaid was arguing that you cannot fix a house with twenty years of termite damage in two weeks. And he was right. The termites represent atherosclerosis, the chronic plaque in the arteries. You cannot repair that structural damage overnight.  
But the newer trials suggest the drug *was never trying to repair the wood in the first place*. It was pumping water out of a flooded basement.\[7\]\[9\]\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]

The **flooded basement is heart failure** - the severe fluid congestion that places mechanical stress on the heart and other organs. Pumping water out of a basement can happen quickly. Likewise, the drug relieves the mechanical workload on a failing heart by reducing the fluid volume it has to pump.\[7\]\[9\]\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]  
So the rapid speed of benefit is biologically plausible. The benefit is simply occurring, and this is an oversimplification, through a **decongestion mechanism** rather than, or to a greater degree than, an **anti-plaque mechanism**.\[7\]\[9\]\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]

That means the critique was right about the timeline problem, but *wrong to assume the only possible mechanism was plaque reversal*.

The post-EMPA-REG data **vindicates the biology of rapid decongestion**. Keeping vulnerable patients out of the hospital by removing excess fluid is a real and measurable effect.\[7\]\[9\]\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]

Anther major flaw in the original trial was in **how the original trial data was collected and analyzed**. And this is where the defense of the original EMPA-REG mortality claim **still falls short**. The new trials do nothing to fix those structural flaws.

As a reminder, these were the other four major cracks.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image42.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.69028in" />

Figure : The other 4 cracks in the Empa-reg trial

**First,** the **mystery deaths**. Nearly 40% of the cardiovascular deaths in the original trial were **non-assessable.\[**4\]\[10\] The adjudication committee did not have the necessary medical files or death certificates. They effectively **inferred** that the patients died of cardiovascular causes. When independent FDA reviewers removed those unproven deaths from the math, **the 38% mortality benefit disappeared.**\[4\]\[10\]

**Second,** the geographic skew. 61% of the trial participants lived in Europe and North America, yet those *patients saw no significant reduction in cardiovascular death*. The entire statistical miracle was generated by smaller cohorts in Asia and Latin America.\[4\]\[10\] That defies any obvious biological explanation. A drug does not decide to stop working when it crosses the Atlantic.

**Third,** the stroke paradox. Patients taking the drug lowered their blood pressure, and lower blood pressure should, by every standard expectation, reduce stroke risk. Yet the patients on the drug had **numerically more strokes**.\[1\]\[4\]

**Fourth,** the **dose-response failure**. In pharmacology, higher doses are generally expected to produce stronger effects. If someone drinks three double espressos, the physiological response should be stronger than with one cup of green tea. Yet in EMPA-REG, the 25mg and 10mg doses produced virtually identical survival curves.\[1\]\[4\]\[12\] That violates basic pharmacological expectations.

A careful examination of **the post-EMPA-REG evidence does not resolve those four anomalies**. They are *idiosyncratic to that one trial*. The new studies, conducted in different populations with different monitoring boards, simply stepped around those original flaws *without ever answering them*.  
That raises a troubling question. If the heart failure and kidney trials failed to replicate the 38% mortality drop (they were not designed to do so, understandably), and the original trial remains riddled with mystery deaths and geographic implausibilities, ***why did the medical community never revoke the original golden headline?***

To answer that, it helps to look at the boardroom logic of modern clinical trials.  
A clinical trial is not just a scientific experiment. It is also **a massive financial investment**. The sponsors had already secured a blockbuster, multibillion-dollar narrative with the 38% reduction claim. Running a new trial designed purely to test mortality would cost hundreds of millions of dollars and carry ***catastrophic risk.*** If that trial came back showing no benefit -which the later hazard ratios suggest it very well might - it would destroy the drug’s biggest selling point.

So, as we saw earlier, the rational corporate move is to pivot. You design trials for populations **where fluid overload is the main threat**, such as heart failure. And you use composite endpoints, bundling hospitalizations with death, so that the trial has enough statistical power to succeed *based largely on the large number of prevented hospital visits*.\[15\]\[16\]\[18\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image43.png" style="width:1.15486in;height:1.90069in" />The composite endpoint becomes a kind of *corporate shield*. It delivers positive press releases and new approvals while pushing mortality into a secondary category where the data may never be strong enough to decisively succeed or fail.\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]\[18\]

We saw earlier in the report, this is where a *conflict-of-interest traffic-light* framework becomes illuminating. As a reminder, **Green** means fully independent: no pharma money involved. **Amber** means mixed funding or academic opinion leaders with consulting ties. **Red** means the research was directly funded, designed, and sponsored by the pharmaceutical company selling the drug.  
And when that framework is applied to the major post-EMPA-REG evidence - EMPEROR-Reduced, EMPEROR-Preserved, EMPA-KIDNEY, and related analyses- the color **is solid red**. Apart from being funded by Boehringer Ingelheim and Eli Lilly, the same companies that manufacture and sell empagliflozin \[18\]\[19\], the authors of the publications were either company employees or recipients of funds from these companies in one way or another, in almost all cases.

Now, regarding the matter of study design, to be fair, the use of composite endpoints in heart failure and kidney trials **is standard regulatory practice.**\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]\[18\]

It is **not illegal**, and it is *not necessarily evidence of a sinister plot*. But it *is excellent corporate risk management.* The new trials flooded the literature with positive, statistically significant data about preventing hospitalizations. And in that flood of positive composite data, the deeply concerning flaws of the original mortality claim were quietly paved over - unaddressed, unreplicated, **but preserved in official medical guidelines.**

So what remains is a **striking split reality**.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image44.png" style="width:5.53785in;height:3.11271in" />

Figure : The Importance of not playing fast and loose with the data

On one hand, there is a drug that **genuinely works** as an advanced water pill. It flushes out fluid, relieves mechanical stress on the heart, and slows kidney decline. And lowers your blood sugar. That is clinically useful.

On the other hand, **the legend** of the drug - the earth-shattering miracle cure for cardiovascular death- is built on a foundation of *unproven mystery deaths*, *bizarre geographic skews*, and *a dose-response failure* that **was never successfully repeated**.

That is the duality. The post-EMPA-REG evidence weakens the idea that the drug is a total mirage, but it strongly validates the warning about statistical illusion. The miracle was a mirage, **even if the decongestion benefit is real.**

And that matters for ordinary people, as well as for payors and those writing medical guidelines, because it rewires how medical news should be consumed.

When a headline screams, “New drug reduces risk of death by 38%,” the medical establishment often treats that as gospel. But as this case suggests, a positive trial in modern medicine is often *a carefully constructed composite of lesser events.* Preventing a hospitalization is valuable, but it is not the same thing as **delivering a mortality miracle.**

Understanding that boardroom logic also changes how one should think about **medical consensus**.  
Doctors are extraordinarily busy. They are not reading four-hundred-page appendices or minutes of FDA meetings between patient appointments. **They follow official clinical guidelines published by medical societies.** And that leads to one final, sobering statistic from the conflict-of-interest mapping: **a striking proportion of clinical guideline committee chairs** (\>70%) have financial ties to the pharmaceutical industry.\[20\] In one study, a survey of guideline authors found **that 87% had ties with drug companies**, and **59% had relationships with companies whose drugs were considered in the guidelines they helped create** .\[21\]

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image45.png" style="width:5.75078in;height:3.17189in" />

Figure : The inherent danger within Guidelines

That matters because a clinical guideline is not merely a friendly suggestion. **It establishes the standard of care.** If a doctor studies the critique, notices the twelve-to-eleven FDA vote, sees the missing mortality confirmation in newer trials, and decides not to prescribe the drug despite guideline support, that doctor may expose themselves to malpractice risk simply for deviating from the formal standard.  
So the guideline can effectively force the doctor’s hand, regardless of the cracks in the evidence base.  
Which leaves one final question worth sitting with:

If deeply conflicted committees codify a drug as the mandatory standard of care based heavily on sponsor-funded composite endpoints and an unreplicated mortality claim from a single flawed trial, does that standard of care truly exist to protect patients?

Or does it also serve as a legal and institutional shield that protects market dominance from independent scrutiny?

## 

# What this means for doctors and patients

One of the most unsettling aspects of this entire story is that most doctors are not acting in bad faith. Many are sincerely trying to follow the evidence.

But if the evidence ecosystem itself is saturated with red and amber influences, then clinicians may be working within a consensus that feels objective while actually being heavily shaped by **sponsor-funded reinforcement.**

That creates a profound self-awareness gap. Doctors may believe they are practicing purely evidence-based medicine, while remaining largely unaware of how much of the evidentiary superstructure has been shaped by *financial incentives, selective emphasis,* and *institutional momentum.*

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image46.png" style="width:6.12079in;height:3.94657in" />

Figure : The Cage of Evidence-based Medicine.

So when a patient receives a prescription based on a celebrated trial and a high-level guideline, the real question is not whether the doctor means well. The question is whether the structure beneath that recommendation is as solid and independent as everyone assumes.

## 

# The deeper lesson

The larger lesson from EMPA-REG is **not that every positive trial is false**, nor that **every regulator is compromised**, nor that **every guideline is worthless**.

It is something more disciplined and more demanding than that.

It is that we should be very cautious when we encounter:

- biological effects **that happen implausibly fast**,

- headline benefits built on **uncertain classifications**,

- major **regional inconsistencies**,

- **paradoxes between fatal and non-fatal outcomes**,

- absent **dose-response relationships**,

- post hoc **explanatory theories**,

- and dense layers of **sponsor-funded “clarification” after the fact**.

When all of those signals appear together, skepticism is not cynicism. It is **good scientific hygiene**.

And perhaps the most provocative question of all is this:

If a medical jury of experts is split **12 to 11** on whether a drug truly deserves a major mortality claim, why does the system move so quickly from **contested uncertainty** to **confident global standard**?

Does evidence-based medicine, in such moments, **primarily protect patients**? Or does it sometimes **protect the institutions and commercial interests** that have already won the narrative battle?

# Summary

So that is the unified picture.

A trial announced as a miracle. A survival benefit that arrived too quickly for the underlying biology. A result resting in part on non-assessable deaths and uneven regional performance. Mechanistic explanations supplied after the fact. A razor-thin regulatory vote. And a vast ecosystem of sponsor-linked papers and guidelines helping convert uncertainty into orthodoxy.

The key lesson is simple: **always inspect the foundation**.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image47.png" style="width:6.6in;height:4.28681in" />

Figure : When the Foundation is compromised.

When the foundation is strong, scrutiny only strengthens confidence. But when the foundation is compromised, the structures built on top of it, including **regulatory approval, expert consensus, and legal standards of care, may become far less trustworthy than they appear.

# Why This Report Matters for Key Stakeholders

<span id="_Toc225996024" class="anchor"></span>**For Regulators**  
This case is a reminder that approval under uncertainty and later clinical certainty are not the same thing. Where a decision rests on a narrow, uneasy, or heavily debated evidentiary base, the risk is not only regulatory error, but narrative over-interpretation after approval.

<span id="_Toc225996025" class="anchor"></span>**For Ethics Committees**  
The report highlights the importance of asking not only whether a study meets formal ethical requirements, but whether the wider interpretive environment may later magnify confidence beyond what the data can comfortably bear. Ethical review does not end with trial conduct; it also has downstream consequences for how evidence is used.

<span id="_Toc225996026" class="anchor"></span>**For Pharmacovigilance Teams**  
When a treatment narrative hardens too quickly, safety signals, unresolved anomalies, or awkward findings may receive less interpretive weight than they deserve. Pharmacovigilance functions best when it is permitted to remain analytically awkward, rather than being forced to fit a commercial success story.

<span id="_Toc225996027" class="anchor"></span>**For Regulatory Affairs Teams**  
This report underscores the difference between obtaining an approval and securing durable scientific legitimacy. A narrow or uneasy regulatory win may still leave unresolved tensions that later require careful handling, especially if post-approval communications begin to sound more definitive than the original record justified.

<span id="_Toc225996028" class="anchor"></span>**For Clinical Development Teams**  
The empagliflozin case illustrates the long-term cost of leaving major mechanistic or evidentiary questions only partially resolved. A stronger confirmatory architecture early on may prevent years of downstream narrative repair, post hoc explanation, and lingering credibility strain.

<span id="_Toc225996029" class="anchor"></span>**For Marketing Teams**  
The report is a caution against allowing a compelling headline to outrun the evidentiary record beneath it. Messages that are commercially effective in the short term can create reputational and ethical liabilities later if the full literature turns out to be more qualified, more contingent, or more conflicted than the campaign implied.

<span id="_Toc225996030" class="anchor"></span>**For CEOs and Senior Leadership**  
This is not just a scientific issue. It is a governance issue. When corporate success becomes tied to a simplified clinical story, the organization may inherit hidden long-tail risks across regulation, credibility, litigation, medical affairs, and public trust. The real strategic question is not only whether a narrative can win, but whether it can remain defensible under sustained scrutiny.

# Appendix 03: The Murky Water in the Healthcare Fishtank

The main report examined a specific case: empagliflozin’s reported cardiovascular benefit, the tensions surrounding that claim, and the institutional processes that helped solidify its narrative. That analysis stands on its own, but it *also sits within a broader context.*

Healthcare evidence is shaped not only by data, but by publication incentives, commercial priorities, regulatory thresholds, expert networks, and professional norms. These structures often support real progress. They can also allow **uncertainty to be overstated**, **selective findings** to **gain prominence**, and **provisional claims** to harden into **accepted wisdom**.

This appendix explores that wider environment. “Murky Water in the Healthcare Fishtank” is a metaphor for system conditions that can distort judgment **without requiring bad faith from every participant**. Weak decisions or overstated confidence may reflect not only individual choices, but also the evidentiary environment in which those choices are made.

The point is not to argue that any single conclusion in this report is necessarily false, nor to indict medicine or pharmaceutical science as a whole. It is to show **why system context matters** when evaluating apparently decisive claims.

Ansilo Data Labs treats the evidence ecosystem itself as part of the analytical record.

Figure 44: When reviewing data, context matters

The figures that follow highlight recurring pressures that can shape clinical narratives: selective publication, post hoc reinforcement, legal and commercial incentives, expert-network dependence, and the gap between formal acceptance and independent certainty. They are offered as context, not proof that any one claim is invalid.

Their relevance to the empagliflozin case is straightforward: if the evidence ecosystem can reward coherence faster than caution, then strong claims deserve proportionate scrutiny. This appendix is included to help the reader understand that setting before drawing conclusions about any single therapeutic narrative.

This figure is intended to illustrate **a structural risk** rather than a claim about any single company or product. Commercial pressure does not operate only at the point of sale. It can also influence what is emphasized, what is downplayed, how safety signals are communicated, and how aggressively favorable interpretations are defended. The relevance here is not that financial incentives always override patient protection, but that where sales growth, market share, and shareholder expectations are strong, the handling of evidence may become less neutral than it appears. In that setting, concealed data, misleading promotion, or inducements to shape prescribing are not random deviations, but recurring forms of pressure that can distort the evidentiary environment itself.

When similar failures recur across firms and products, they point to structural pressures that deserve analytical attention. These examples are relevant not because they collapse all cases into one, but because they remind the reader that evidence is interpreted within institutions that face legal, financial, and reputational incentives.

A single case can be dismissed as an outlier. A sequence of cases across firms, products, and therapeutic areas is harder to ignore. The importance of these examples lies less in the individual scandals than in the recurring pattern they reveal.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image58.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.64236in" />

The purpose of this matrix is *comparative* rather than accusatory. Different products and firms may fail in different ways, but the underlying pattern often rhymes: concealed or selectively reported data, off-label promotion, kickbacks, risk minimization, and delayed regulatory correction. By placing these cases side by side, the figure makes visible a broader analytical point: **the problem is not confined to one product, one company, or one therapeutic area.** When similar categories of distortion recur across settings, they justify greater caution about how confidently a dominant medical narrative should be accepted without examining the institutional conditions through which it was built and stabilized.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image59.png" style="width:6.15689in;height:3.38875in" />

Figure 48: Merck is included here as an example of how high-confidence therapeutic narratives can persist despite serious evidentiary and safety concerns.

The Vioxx case is relevant not because it proves all later concerns are equivalent, but because it shows how commercial success and clinical confidence can coexist **for a time** with unresolved safety questions. Merck withdrew Vioxx from the market in 2004 after data from the APPROVe trial showed an increased risk of cardiovascular events, and the company later entered a roughly \$950 million criminal and civil resolution with the U.S. government related to the drug’s promotion. The broader lesson is not product-specific guilt by association. It is that *persuasive adoption curves* and *strong market narratives* **do not**, by themselves, guarantee that the underlying safety picture is as settled as it appears.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image60.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.68819in" />

Figure 49: GSK illustrates how commercial pressure can affect the communication of risk and benefit in ways that later require legal or regulatory correction.

This figure is most useful when read as an example of **how risk communication itself** can become part of the problem. In its 2012 resolution, the U.S. government stated that between 2001 and 2007 GSK **failed to include certain Avandia safety data in annual reports to the FDA**, including information connected to post-marketing studies and European concerns about cardiovascular safety. FDA labeling for rosiglitazone products also carried prominent warnings relating to congestive heart failure and myocardial ischemia. Taken together, these elements illustrate the report’s broader analytical point: **headline confidence** and underlying **evidentiary quality** ***can diverge***, especially where commercial incentives and safety communication come into tension.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image61.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.67639in" />

Figure 50: Pfizer is included as a further illustration of how legal and commercial pressures can intersect with scientific and promotional claims.

The Bextra case shows how **promotional conduct** can move beyond interpretation into the active expansion of prescribing beyond approved evidentiary boundaries. In 2009, Pfizer and its subsidiary Pharmacia & Upjohn agreed to a \$2.3 billion resolution, including a felony misbranding plea tied to Bextra, civil allegations involving several other products, and allegations that kickbacks were used to induce prescribing. The FDA had not approved certain promoted uses and doses of Bextra because of safety concerns, and Bextra was later withdrawn from the market. The reason this matters in the present appendix is contextual: it illustrates how **commercial systems can reinforce uptake even where the approved evidence base does not support the breadth of real-world promotion**.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image62.png" style="width:6.6in;height:3.68958in" />

Figure 51: Johnson & Johnson illustrates how broad institutional credibility can coexist with serious failures in risk communication or product stewardship.

This figure is intended to highlight the heightened stakes when disputed promotional practices **involve vulnerable populations**. In 2013, Johnson & Johnson and its subsidiaries agreed to pay more than \$2.2 billion to resolve criminal and civil allegations relating in part to Risperdal, including promotion for uses not approved as safe and effective and the payment of kickbacks. The analytical point here is not simply that misconduct occurred, but that **a product can retain institutional legitimacy while safety communication, labeling, and prescribing boundaries lag behind the realities of risk**. That is one of the recurring conditions in which **narrative friction** becomes possible: **confidence** travels through the system **faster than caution**. included because they show how difficult it can be for correction to keep pace with narrative momentum.

Figure 52: Purdue is included as an example of how powerful clinical narratives can be sustained despite profound downstream harm.

The Purdue case is relevant here because it shows how persuasive product messaging can continue shaping clinical behavior even when the underlying risk picture is far less benign than advertised. In 2007, Purdue Frederick and three executives pleaded guilty in connection with the misbranding of OxyContin, and the company agreed to pay more than \$600 million, while the executives paid an additional \$34.5 million. The government’s case centered on claims that OxyContin had been marketed to clinicians as less addictive, less prone to abuse, and less likely to cause withdrawal than other opioids. The significance of the example, in the context of this appendix, is not merely historical. It is that **commercial storytelling** can become powerful enough to influence prescribing culture long after the **evidentiary basis for confidence** should have been treated more cautiously.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image64.png" style="width:6.05293in;height:3.32007in" />

Figure 53: Sanofi-Aventis adds to the cross-case pattern by showing that evidentiary distortion is not confined to promotion alone but can extend to the integrity of the clinical record itself.

The Ketek episode matters because it illustrates a different failure mode within the same broad ecosystem: not simply aggressive marketing, but **compromised trial integrity** and **delayed correction.** Congressional and regulatory scrutiny of Ketek focused heavily on Study 3014, which became controversial after serious allegations of fraud by a principal investigator, Anne Kirkman-Campbell. FDA materials later reflected major safety concerns as well, including severe hepatotoxicity, with labeling narrowed so that telithromycin was left indicated for community-acquired pneumonia rather than the broader set of earlier uses. In this appendix, the case functions as a reminder that the apparent strength of a medical narrative can be undermined not only by spin **after** the fact, but **by weaknesses in the evidentiary substrate on which later confidence was built**.

The appendix now shifts from enforcement examples to the mechanisms through which distortion can become normalized. The issue is not only overt misconduct, but **the quieter ways in which conflicted interpretation can become routine**.

<img src="/mnt/data/empagliflozin_report_final_final01_media/media/image65.png" style="width:6.07371in;height:3.36533in" />

Figure 54: Novartis is included as another example of how legal, promotional, and evidentiary pressures can converge in ways that later demand scrutiny.

The Novartis example is useful because it shows that distortion need not take the form of a false headline claim alone; it can also arise through **the commercial engineering of prescribing and refill behavior.** In one major settlement, Novartis agreed to pay \$390 million to resolve allegations that it provided kickbacks to specialty pharmacies in connection with Exjade and Myfortic, including conduct alleged to encourage refill and switching behavior. In a separate matter, the government alleged that Novartis used sham speaker programs to induce prescribing of drugs including Lotrel and Valturna. The broader relevance here is cumulative: these cases show how commercial systems can shape what gets reinforced in practice, **even where the mechanism is quieter than overt fraud**. The result is an evidentiary environment in which uptake, repetition, and institutional confidence may be influenced by incentives that deserve explicit examination rather than silent assumption.

Funding relationships, consultancy arrangements, recurring authorship networks, and institutional dependence can all shape how uncertainty is interpreted and communicated.

This is why conflict of interest matters analytically even when it does not, by itself, invalidate a conclusion: it can affect which explanations are amplified, which doubts are minimized, and which narratives become durable.

The final pages of the appendix bring the argument together. Distortion in healthcare is rarely a single act; more often it is the result of repeated framing, selective visibility, institutional reinforcement, and the gradual conversion of uncertainty into confidence.

When uncertainty is compressed into confidence too quickly, the resulting harm may be borne by patients, practitioners, and health systems.

The purpose of the “Narrative Friction” framework is not to reject consensus reflexively, but to examine whether the confidence attached to a claim is fully proportionate to the strength, clarity, and independence of the underlying record.
